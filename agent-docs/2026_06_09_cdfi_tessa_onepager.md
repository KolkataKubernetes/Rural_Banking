# Community Development Financial Institutions

Community Development Financial Institutions (CDFIs) occupy a somewhat different position in the small business finance landscape than either banks or credit unions, but they should not be treated as a wholly separate lending sector. CDFI status is a federal certification applied to mission-driven financial institutions that serve underserved markets and populations. Those institutions can take several forms, including loan funds, banks, credit unions, and bank holding companies. As a result, some share of CDFI activity is likely already reflected elsewhere in this report, particularly in the sections on banking and credit unions. For that reason, the role of this section is not to present CDFI lending as additive to the other lending totals in the report, but rather to recognize CDFIs as a distinct mission-oriented layer within Wisconsin’s broader financial system.

CDFIs are designed to expand access to credit in places and populations that are commonly underserved by conventional financial markets. In practice, that mission can make them an important complement to the banking and credit union activity described above, particularly where financing decisions depend on community knowledge, relationships, and a willingness to serve borrowers that may not fit standardized underwriting models. At the same time, the available public data impose meaningful limits on what can be said with precision. The public institution-level CDFI release does not include institution names or street addresses, and the public transaction-level release is difficult to reconcile cleanly across years in the period surrounding 2016 and 2017. These constraints suggest a narrow and descriptive treatment is most appropriate.

Figure CDFI-1 shows the county distribution of unique Wisconsin-based CDFIs identified in the public institution-level release. Because the public ILR provides ZIP codes rather than exact addresses, the map should be interpreted as a county-assigned location measure rather than a precise headquarters map. Even with that limitation, the geography is informative. We identify 22 unique Wisconsin-based CDFIs in the public ILR between 2003 and 2015. Observed CDFI presence is concentrated in Milwaukee County, with smaller clusters in Vilas and Dane counties and single institutions distributed across several other counties. The visual therefore supports a simple but useful point: Wisconsin’s CDFI footprint is real, but spatially uneven, with activity concentrated in a relatively small number of places.

The public institution-level data also indicate that CDFIs play a nontrivial role in Wisconsin business finance, even if they are not a dominant channel. In the 2015 ILR cross-section, we identify 12 Wisconsin-based CDFIs, of which 9 reported any business originations. Together, those institutions reported 814 business originations totaling approximately $154.8 million. These totals are large enough to indicate that CDFIs are meaningful participants in the state’s business lending environment. At the same time, because some CDFIs are depository institutions, these figures should not be read as lending activity that sits wholly outside the banking and credit union totals already documented elsewhere in the report. Instead, they are best understood as evidence that a meaningful share of Wisconsin small business lending is delivered through institutions with an explicit mission to reach underserved borrowers and communities.

Taken together, the evidence suggests that CDFIs warrant inclusion in the report, but as a short offset section rather than a parallel lending chapter. They provide an institutional mechanism for directing capital toward underserved places and populations, and their presence in Wisconsin is substantial enough to merit attention. However, given both the overlap with other lender types and the limits of the public data, the most defensible presentation is a concise section built around location, role, and scale rather than a long standalone time series.
